Survey says...

  • Most "practicing Christians" in America - defined as those who attend church regularly and say that their faith is important to them - hold to elements of non-Christian worldviews. Only 17% look at the world through a predominantly Biblical perspective.

    • (Barna survey article, May 2017)

  • 61% hold to elements of "New Spirituality," i.e., New Age, Eastern, or Neo-Pagan beliefs (such as all religions being one; the karmic view that if we do good we receive good, and vice versa).
  • 54% have postmodernist views that truth and morality are relative.
  • 36% hold to Marxist views, such as the evils of private property and the desirability of government control.
  • 29% agree with secularism, on materialism, and science.

Many Christians don't act like Christians because they don't think like Christians. Why?

Problem: lack of accurate biblical teaching that provides framework of biblical worldview...How can that be? Answer:

Survey indicates that only half of protestant/evangelical pastors have a solid biblical worldview.

Absolute truth

Biblical ethics rests on the belief in absolute truth...What's that?

However, many professing Christians accept the prevalent attitude of our society that truth is relative to the person and the situation, choosing which laws of God to accept.

The dominant culture bases moral choices on whatever will bring the most pleasing or satisfying results for the individual.

Christian commitment

Faith commitment affects one's perspective on moral issues...but the effect isn't as strong as it should be.

Most adults say they're concerned about the moral condition of our nation, but a large % holds to views that conflict with the moral teaching of their professed faith.

How do most Christians in America see themselves in American culture?

Many Christians think of themselves as individuals first, then Americans, and as Christians third.

More recently there are indications of a reverse trend: there is a wider disparity, but teens and young adults who are committed Christians, are strongly committed.

Ethics and education

Ethics may be taught in public schools; but teachers avoid discussing ethical issues and usually teach that ethics are relative, situational, individual.

Ethics must be based on something more concrete than personal preference.

We face difficult moral issues which need analysis based on an ethical system that provides clear and consistent direction.
