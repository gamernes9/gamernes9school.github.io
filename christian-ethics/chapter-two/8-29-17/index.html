History: Classical Greek Philosophers

Plato (ca. 428 - 346 B.C.)

  • Student of Socrates, teacher of Aristotle
  • Taught a theory of knowledge (epistemology), a theory of conduct (ethics), a theory of government (political science), a theory of universe (cosmology)
  • More discourse on virtue than moral philosophy
  • Greeks: greatest good of man is happiness
  • Means to practice virtue and attain wisdom
  • The state should have unlimited authority
  • No private property
  • No family institutions...children belong to the state and should be educated by state-appointed officials, according to ability
  • Contrast with Christian ethics: Plato is...man-centered...
    • Right and wrong is judged by whatever was reasonable to the individual...action judged ethical if a rational justification was provided

Aristotle (384 - 322 B.C.)

  • Student of Plato, tutored Alexander the Great
  • Accepted Plato's concept of the universe as an ideal world and interrelated whole
  • Conclusions based on both inductive (scientific observation) method and deductive (logical reasoning) method
  • Chief goal of life: goodness or happiness, which comes from "self-realization"
  • Assumed goodness of human beings
  • Classical Greek philosophy maintained that reason would guide people to be good and live in peace
  • Contrast with Christian ethics:
    • Bible teaches that highest goal is to know, love, obey God
    • Bible teaches that human beings are "created in God's image" and capable of love; but both Bible and experience/history teach that humans are inclined to and do rebel against God

History: Medieval Philosophers

Augustine (396 - 430 A.D.)

  • Wove NT teaching and platonic tradition
  • Struggled with Pelagian heresy (which denied doctrine of original sin), and Manicheanism (dualism of good and evil)
  • His theology and ethics are still influential
  • Shifted focus from happiness and goodness to doing good and following biblical commands
  • Because of the effect of sin on humans, he affirmed need of God's grace
  • Developed a social ethic in The City of God, teaching that there are two different communities - the city of God and the city of man
  • Formalized the concept of separating church and state
