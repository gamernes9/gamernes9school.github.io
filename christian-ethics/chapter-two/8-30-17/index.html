Thomas Aquinas (ca. 1224 - 1274)

  • Systematized Latin (Roman) theology
  • Faced challenges of scientific rationalism, technology, and progress, along with changing perspectives
  • Maintained that social institutions could encourage the development of "good people"
  • Maintained that the purpose of the law was to mold good people, not merely restrain evil
  • Distinguished between four kinds of law:
    • Eternal law - Divine Reason governs the whole community (physical laws such as gravity)
    • Natural law - good is to be done and evil, avoided; principles which are considered to be self-evident
    • Human law - civil and criminal laws that govern communities
    • Divine law - God's special plans for humanity, revealed through the Bible

David Hume (1711 - 1776)

  • Empiricism: theories should be based only on what is perceived through senses
  • Hume took it to logical extreme of radical skepticism, maintaining that our knowledge is only a series of sensations & can't be certain
  • Proposed that morality is only a matter of sense impressions, or mental perceptions
  • Argued that morality should be judged by its usefulness/utility (utilitarianism)

Immanuel Kant (1724 - 1804)

  • At height of the Enlightenment, proposed an ethical system in which reason is the final authority for morality
  • Only objects of experience (phenomena) may be known; objects beyond our experience (noumena) are unknowable
  • Actions must be from sense of duty dictated by reason
